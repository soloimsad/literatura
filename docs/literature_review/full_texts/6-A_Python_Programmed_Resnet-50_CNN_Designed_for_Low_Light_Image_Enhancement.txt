Abstract—Enhancing the quality of photos taken in low light 
is the goal of the computer vision problem known as Low-Light 
Image Enhancement. Increasing the brightness, clarity, and 
visual attractiveness of images while minimizing noise and 
distortion is the aim of low -light image enhancement . Most 
CNN-based methods have complex network structures and the 
network consists of several sub-networks. The challenges posed 
by insufficient illumination in image enhancement with their 
visual quality low light condition often lead to reduced visibility 
and loss of important details, impacting various applications in 
autonomous system. In contrast to these problems we utilize 
ResNet-50 for image segmentation up to 50 layers to reduce 
noises in image, scattering of images and more focusing on 
higher brightness places in an image. Python programming is 
used to restoring edges and textures and improving overall 
image visibility. According to our experimental results, the 
proposed method achieves improvised image in clarity and 
environmental issues such as fog, pollution.

II. EXISTING SYSTEM 
Inadequate illumination can have an impact on photos that 
are taken in low light. A digital image processing method 
called low -light image enhancement, or LLIE, transforms 
low-light photographs into typical, natural-light ones. LLIE's 
three primary goals are to replicate natural brightness and 
colour, preserve edges and textures, and minimize noise and 
artifacts. Deep learning-based methods that can achieve high 
performance have been the focus of numerous sorts of 
research in recent years. The lengthy inference time caused by 
intricate network topologies is the main issue with these 
approaches, though.  
In order to address the trade -off between implementation 
time and performance, they present a straightforward network 
with efficient modules. To maintain edges and textures, pre -
processing is used and we use a U-Net structure. To preserve 
high-frequency details, our method integrates Pixel Shuffler, 
while Channel Attention mechanisms help enhances  color 
fidelity and illumination. Additionally, we incorporate a Res 
FFT-ReLU block to effectively suppress noise. Experimental 
evaluations demonstrate that our approach surpasses 
traditional low-light image enhancement (LLIE) methods in 
both speed and overall performance. 
By controlling the effect of classic histogram equalization 
(HE), the dynamic histogram equalization (DHE) technology 
enhances an image without sacrificing any of its nuances. 
Dynamic Histogram Equalization (DHE) improves contrast 
by segmenting the image histogram at local minima and then 
performing histogram equalization independently within each 
segment, allowing better preservation of image details. To 
make sure there are no dominating sections, these partitions 
are also subjected to a repartitioning test. This technique 
performs better than other current methods because it 
improves contrast without producing unwanted artifacts or 
serious negative effects like washed -out appearance or 
checkerboard effects. 
This study investigates the optimal skid height and landing 
safety of an aerial vehicle operating on sloped terrain by 
analyzing the surface angle of the landscape. Advanced 
computer vision techniques such as Canny edge detection and 
Haar cascades are employed to identify objects and ex tract 
landscape boundaries from captured images. The redesigned 
landing skid aims to enhance the aerial vehicle's ability to land 
securely and efficiently on uneven ground. 
Typically, little information is available to improve photos 
taken in poor light. To obtain trustworthy information for the 
augmentation, we suggest taking advantage of the interactions 
between superposed luminance levels. A noise -resilient 
transformation is then used after Grover's method executes the 
interaction that causes information to flow between brightness 
levels. Based on both qualitative and quantitative data, our 
concept improves low light images better than the state-of-the-
art. 
III. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY 
Although photos taken in low light have poor visual 
quality, enhancing photographs in low light is a major 
difficulty. We address this by treating low-light enhancement 
as a residual learning problem, with the goal of estimating the 
difference (residual) between photos taken in normal light and 
those taken in low light. In order to improve low-light picture 
enhancement, we present a unique Convolutional Neural 
Network (CNN) based on the ResNet50 architecture in this 
study.  
Although it is classified as a low -level image processing 
activity, low-light enhancement is very different from tasks 
like image denoising and super resolution. Pixel values in 
deteriorated photos are usually near to their true values in 
super resolution and denoising, with just slight variations in 
average pixel values. Low -light enhancement, on the other 
hand, results in images with higher noise and less contrast. As 
a result, we create a customized ResNet50 architecture for 
improving low-light photos, e mphasizing the acquisition of 
complex low-light-specific properties. 
A. Algorithm  
CNNs, such as the ResNet-50 architecture, are widely used 
for object classification tasks involving multiple categories. 
Rather than relying on clustering, these models learn complex 
hierarchical features through convolutional layers. CNNs are 
often considered translation -invariant due to their shared -
weight structure and receptive field mechanism. Their 
versatility makes them suitable for a range of tasks including 
image and vi deo recognition, medical imaging, natural 
language processing, and recommendation systems.  
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) can be viewed as 
a regularized form of multilayer perceptrons (MLPs). While 
MLPs are typically fully connected —where each neuro n in 
one layer connects to every neuron in the next —this full 
connectivity often leads to over fitting. Traditional 
regularization techniques such as weight decay and dropout 
are commonly applied to mitigate this. CNNs, however, 
achieve a different form of regularization by leveraging local 
connectivity and shared weights. They capture hierarchical 
patterns in data by learning simple features in early layers and 
progressively combining them into more complex 
representations. As a result, CNNs exhibit lower parameter 
complexity and reduced risk of over fitting compared to fully 
connected networks. 
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are inspired by 
biological processes, particularly the visual perception 
mechanisms found in animals. Although the precise 
correspondence between artificial neuron connections and the 
animal visual cortex is not fully understood, CNNs mimic the 
concept of receptive fields. In the visual cortex, a receptive 
field refers to the specific region of the input space where a 
neuron responds. These fields often overlap, enabling the full 
visual field to be represented through the combined activity of 
many neurons. 
CNNs require a relatively lower amount of pre-processing 
than other image classification techniques. In conventional 
systems, the convolution kernels or filters are manually built; 
this suggests that the network learns to optimize them.  One

Convolutional filters in CNNs are defined by several 
hyperparameters, including their height, width, and the 
number of input and output channels. One key constraint is 
that the depth of the filter (i.e., the number of input channels) 
must match the depth of the input feature map. This ensures 
that each filter process es all available channels of the input 
data correctly. 
The convolution operation in neural networks relies on 
key hyperparameters such as padding and stride. After 
applying the convolution, each layer passes the resulting 
feature maps to the next. This mec hanism mimics how 
neurons in the visual cortex respond to specific regions of the 
visual field —referred to as receptive fields. Each 
convolutional neuron processes only the input within its 
receptive region, enabling localized feature extraction. Unlike 
fully connected networks, which are parameter -heavy and 
inefficient for image data, convolutional networks are more 
suitable for processing photographs due to their spatial 
locality and weight sharing. 
Processing image data with fully connected networks is 
inefficient due to the high dimensionality —each pixel in an 
image represents a unique input feature. For instance, a fully 
connected layer handling a 100×100 pixel image would 
require 10,000 weights pe r neuron, resulting in a massive 
number of parameters. Convolutional layers address this by 
significantly reducing parameter count through local 
connectivity and weight sharing. A 5×5 filter, for example, 
requires just 25 learnable weights regardless of the image size, 
which makes training more efficient. Additionally, using 
fewer parameters helps reduce overfitting and mitigates issues 
like vanishing or exploding gradients during backpropagation. 
To reduce computational complexity, convolutional 
neural net works often use pooling layers —either local or 
global. These layers downsample feature maps by aggregating 
information from small regions, effectively reducing spatial 
dimensions. Local pooling, such as 2×2 max or average 
pooling, combines nearby activatio ns into a single 
representative value, which helps preserve key features while 
lowering the data size passed to the next layer. 
Global pooling operates across all spatial positions in a 
feature map, summarizing the output of an entire 
convolutional layer. The two most commonly used pooling 
methods are average pooling, which computes the mean of 
values within a region, and max pooling, which selects the 
highest value. In convolutional neural networks, the 
convolutional layer plays a fundamental role. It cons ists of 
trainable filters (or kernels) that slide across the input volume 
with a limited spatial extent, while spanning the full depth of 
the input data.  
During the forward pass in a convolutional neural 
network, each filter slides over the spatial dimens ions of the 
input volume. At each position, the filter computes a dot 
product with the overlapping input region, generating a 
corresponding value in the resulting 2D feature map, also 
known as the activation map. 
 
Fig. 3. Feature Extraction Process 
In a convolutional neural network, each filter is trained to 
respond to specific features at particular spatial locations 
within the input. The output of a convolutional layer consists 
of multiple feature maps, one per filter, which are stacked 
along the depth a xis to form the final output volume. Each 
neuron in a feature map processes a local region of the input 
and shares its weights with others in the same map, allowing 
efficient feature extraction across the spatial dimensions.

B. Proposed Methodology CNN with RESNET 50 
Residual Networks (ResNets) are a type of convolutional 
neural network (CNN) designed to address the vanishing 
gradient problem in deep architectures.  
Widely used in computer vision tasks, ResNets introduce 
shortcut connections that allow gradi ents to flow more 
effectively during training. ResNet -50, a popular variant, 
consists of 50 layers including 48 convolutional layers, one 
max pooling layer, and one average pooling layer. These 
layers are organized into residual blocks that enable the 
construction of very deep networks without degradation in 
performance. 
ResNet-34 is part of the family of residual neural networks 
and includes 34 learnable layers. These models are often 
pretrained on large -scale datasets such as ImageNet, which 
contains over  a million labeled images across various 
categories. A key innovation in ResNet is the use of shortcut 
(or skip) connections, which help mitigate the vanishing 
gradient problem by allowing the network to learn residual 
mappings. Instead of learning the ful l transformation, each 
block learns the difference —or residual—between the input 
and the desired output, enabling the training of much deeper 
networks. 
VGG architectures such as VGG -16 and VGG -19 utilize 
uniform 3×3 convolutional filters and were influential in early 
deep learning models for image recognition. However, 
ResNets are more computationally efficient. For example, 
while VGG-19 requires approximately 19.6 billion floating -
point operations (FLOPs), a ResNet -34 achieves similar 
performance with only  3.6 billion FLOPs, and ResNet -18 
requires just 1.8 billion FLOPs. This significant reduction in 
computation makes ResNets faster and more scalable for 
deeper architectures 
ResNet-50 comprises fifty levels and five blocks, each of 
which has a set of residual blocks. The residual blocks allow 
information from earlier layers to be preserved, which helps 
the network create better representations of the data it 
receives. ResNet is what you ought to use. It is deep enough, 
with 34, 50, or 101 layers, to begin. A s the hierarchy's depth 
increases, so do the classification accuracy and representation 
capabilities.  
The comparatively shallow CNN is unable to outperform 
random guessing (which has a 50% chance of being correct 
when guessing between two classes) when trained on just 20 
photos per class. ResNet50 is a deep pre-trained network that 
requires only 20 images per class and has an accuracy rate of 
70%.
